Introduction¶

Books and movies are two beloved forms of entertainment that often borrow from one another. In this notebook, I will be examining books that have been made into film adaptations to learn how the quality of a story changes when transfered from page to screen, as measured by user ratings.

Data sources used¶

For this project, I used two datasets. Goodreads is a site that allows users to log books they have read and rate and write reviews for them, among other things. The Goodreads dataset I used is not directly from the Goodreads API, but rather a simplified, more comprehensive, cleaner dataset that was formed from the API and posted on Kaggle. 12,475 books of those that existed on Goodreads as of June 2019 are in this dataset, along with their average user-given ratings.

Click here to view the Goodreads dataset on Kaggle.

MovieLens is a site that provides personalized film recommendations based on how you have rated other films. The version I am using is a stable dataset from 2017 that includes all 45,000 films that were on MovieLens at the time of scraping. There are more recent datasets with more films, but I am using the stable dataset so that anyone can reproduce the results below if they are tempted.

Click here to view the MovieLens dataset on Kaggle.

series_regex = re.compile("^(.+)\s*\(.+#\d{1,}.*\)$")

def get_title(full_title):
    match = series_regex.match(full_title)
    if match:
        return match.group(1)
    else:
        return full_title

# This only works for Western names with a first and last name.
# This doesn't work correctly if the primary author has a hyphen in their name.

The 'authors' attribute in the Kaggle dataset was formatted as a hyphen-delimited string of the authors, translators, illustrators, etc. for a particular book. Unfortunately, this makes it difficult to parse because some people have hyphens in their name. To simplify things, I create a 'primary author' field that takes the first name before any appearance of a hyphen. This rough parsing is sufficient because I only use the author to distinguish books with the same title. In addition, the authors are listed in an arbitrary order, so sometimes an illustrator or translator will take the spot of the 'primary author' even if this is not accurate. Again, it is okay because this column is only used to differentiate books with the same title.

The purpose of extracting the primary author is for the case where a single piece of writing is published or reprinted in different editions over time under different ISBNs. This applies especially to works that have been around for a while (think 19th-century classics and high school English class text). To simplify, I only keep a single entry for each book with the highest rating that any of them received. I distinguish a "book" by a unique (Primary Author, Title) pair.

Normalizing ratings¶

Before we connect and books and movies, I'll normalize book and movie ratings so they can be compared directly. At present, books are scored out of 5 points, while movies are out of 10 points. Since both point systems measure a proportion rather than an absolute value, I'll divide book ratings by 5 and movies by 10 so they will both be on a 0 to 1 scale.

In [109]:
books['average_rating'] = books['average_rating'].apply(lambda x : x / 5)
movies['vote_average'] = movies['vote_average'].apply(lambda x : x / 10)

Connecting books to movie adaptations¶

For some book-movie pairs, the relationship is obvious because they have the same title, or because the relationship is simply well known and well documented in popular culture. For others, the relationship is not so obvious, as book-to-movie adaptations are often given different titles. For example, Steven Spielberg's Schinder's List (1994) is based on an Austrian novel by the name of Schindler's Ark. Sacrificing the size of our dataset for accuracy, I will be looking only at movie adaptations that have the same title as the original books.

This method of record linkage sometimes generates matches between books and movies that are not actually related. For example, Heat is the name of a 1985 novel by William Goldman, as well as of a 2006 novel by Mike Lupica. It is also the name of three separate American movies released in 1972, 1986, and 1995. It happens that the 1986 film is an adaptation of the 1985 novel, but the other two are unrelated to any of the novels and coincidentally share the same name. In order to avoid false matches like this, I will be considering book and movie titles with two or more words in the title. This rules out a lot of well-known titles such as Othello and Hamlet.

Then, there are cases where the book that a movie was made from is ambiguous because there exist multiple candidates with the same title. I will make the assumption that books must be considerably popular if they are chosen to be made into films, so I keep the instance of a title with the greatest number of ratings on Goodreads. I treat the book-movie relationship as a one-to-many relationship.

# Remove books with titles of less than two words

    if minimum_title.search(title):
        return True
    else:
        return False

# Remove books with one-word titles

books = books[books.title_sufficiently_long]
books.drop(labels='title_sufficiently_long', axis=1, inplace=True)

# Do the same thing with movies - remove movies with one-word titles

movies = movies[movies.title_sufficiently_long]
movies.drop(labels='title_sufficiently_long', axis=1, inplace=True)

# If there are multiple books with the same name, keep the one with the most ratings.
books.sort_values(by='ratings_count', ascending=False, inplace=True)
books.drop_duplicates(subset='title', inplace=True)

Adaptations by genre¶

We can count how many adaptations were made of each book and sort them by descending count to see which books have been made into the greatest number of adaptations. We can also assign a genre to each book based on the most common genre that its movie adaptations were assigned, as the Goodreads dataset doesn't include information about book genre. Of course, book and movie genres are slightly different: books are usually just "Fiction/Non-fiction," while fiction books can be made into Drama, Romance, Comedy, etc. films. The purpose is just to give a general descriptive term to the nature of the book.

In [112]:
from collections import Counter

adapt_per_book = []
genre_regex = "{'id': \d{1,}, 'name': '([A-Za-z]+)'}"

# For each film, go through its adaptations by genre and give it the genre label
# that occurs most frequently.
for (title, df) in adaptations.groupby(by='title'):
    genres_for_this_book = []
    adapts_of_this_title = df.genres
    for (_, this_adapts_genres) in adapts_of_this_title.iteritems():
        matches = re.findall(genre_regex, this_adapts_genres)
        genres_for_this_book += matches
    
    c = Counter(genres_for_this_book)
    genre_with_count = c.most_common(1)
    if len(genre_with_count) > 0:
        (genre,_) = genre_with_count[0]
    else:
        genre = 'N/A'
    
    adapt_per_book.append({'title':title,
                           'primary_author':df.iloc[0].primary_author,
                           'num_adapt':df.shape[0],
                           'book_rating':df.average_rating.mean(),
                           'avg_film_rating':df.vote_average.mean(),
                           'avg_film_revenue':df.revenue.mean(),
                           'genre':genre # New field
                          })

adapt_per_book_df = pd.DataFrame(data=adapt_per_book)

There is a noticeable main cluster with a center roughly at (0.8, 0.6). The black line is a linear regression through our data. The red line is a line on which would lie movies that receive the same score as their books. Since the main cluster is positioned below this line, it suggests that viewers don't usually judge movies to be as good as the books they were based on. Why is this? One on hand, it may have to do with the particular production company, director, and budget that happen to take on a book. On the other, it likely has an explanation rooted in sociological and cultural phenomena. While these reasons are not necessarily beyond the scope of data science, they are outside the ability of the particular datasets we are using (Goodreads and MovieLens) to explain.

You can see at the bottom that there are quite a few books with decent ratings that received 0-point movie ratings.

From these individual scatter plots, we can see that there's no particular genre among the five examined that tends to get turned into movies that are as good or better: no genre shows has many points around or above the maroon 'equal ratings' line. In fact, there is a slight negative linear trend for books that are turned into action and comedy movies, suggesting that books of these genres are adapted poorly in comparison to drama, crime, and adventure.

Once again, we see that most movie adaptations have a lower relative score in comparison to their original book, but for indidvidual genres. Some possible reasons are that we tend to be disappointed by movie adaptations because they are simply poorly produced, or that we tend to be harsher when rating movies versus books.

Instead of having the model return a binary classification (True = prediction that book will be or was adapted, False = prediction that book will not be adapted), I used predict_proba, which returns the predicted probabilities that a data point will take on either category. For example, if the model predicts [0.7 0.3] for a given book, that means a 0.7 probability of it being adapted=True and 0.3 probability of adapted=False.

The plot above visualizes a small sample of the test data and predictions (100 out of 8101). The x-axis is not meaningful; it simply measures the location of a data point in the test dataset array. The blue bars at the bottom represent the predicted probability of True. For example, if, at x=20, the blue bar measures 0.4, that means that the model has predicted there to be a 40% chance that that book is made into an adaptation.

The red bar represents if the given book was actually made into an adaptation. If there are data points for which the red bar appears, but the blue bar is not visible (probability true = 0), then the model predicted that the book would not be adapted, whereas in reality it was. If the blue and red bars are both visible for a given data point, and if the blue bar is nearly as high as the red bar, then the model had a more accurate prediction.

Conclusion: Things to take into account¶

In this tutorial, we've explored how book relate to movie adaptations. The biggest challenge in connecting these two domains is entity resolution: with the datasets alone, or even with outside help, it's difficult to automate a process that accurately links movies to books. My simple approach assumes that a movie is adapted from a book when it has the same title of two or more words.

This works a lot of the time, but it also doesn't work a lot of the time. Think of "First Love" as a generic rom com title. It's the English-translated title of six movies in our dataset, but only one of them is an adaptation of Ivan Turgenev's novella of the same name. Most often, the biggest problem is that movie adaptations are given a slightly different title that retains some keywords of the original book title but isn't exactly identical character for character. One idea to improve upon book-movie record linkage is to take an NLP approach to measuring similarity that places less weight on or even understand substitutions of generic words like "the" or "on." This tutorial performs a basic matching that sacrifices accuracy for simplicity, but which does enough to permit us a general idea of what the information we're after.

We looked primarily at how book and movie rankings compare. By performing linear regression, we found that there was a slight positive correlation between book and movie ranking, but that when normalized, movies tend to be rated more poorly, in general and for the specific genres we examined. I believe this difference has less to do with objective "goodness" of either form but rather about the difference in attitude toward books and movies in our culture as well as the gap in translation that results from it.

Once again, my approach is a simplified one that assumes that user's ratings correlate directly with an absolute "goodness" of a book plot or appeal. It's nice to assume that book rankings correlate directly with movie rankings, but in reality, they can almost be considered different entities in their authorship, form, and presentation. The "goodness" of a movie depends on much more than the book it is based on. Some of these dependencies are the director, the production company, the budget, and pure chance of if and which production company takes in interest in it, and such. One approach could be to to assign a "capacity" or "potential" score to each production company based on the average ratings of movies they produce: then, the rating of a movie in production could be predicted based on this score. We might find there to be a clear relationship, or not, since the same production companies might be capable of producing good or bad movies.

Lastly, our interpretation of book/movie excellence relies solely on user ratings. As such, the ratings we've been using are subject to subjective evaluation, personal preference, error in recording, and all the things that come up when our data source is founded in opinion. It's important to understand that while ratings give us a general idea of a movie's quality, popularity, and box office performance, they don't necessarily lend to and might even contradict our personal beliefs about it.
